# Chapter 1: Physical Quantities & Measurement

## Key Concepts

1. **Physical Quantity** — Anything that can be measured (length, mass, time, etc.)
2. **Base Quantities** — 7 fundamental quantities that cannot be derived:
   - Length (meter, m), Mass (kilogram, kg), Time (second, s)
   - Electric Current (ampere, A), Temperature (kelvin, K)
   - Amount of Substance (mole, mol), Luminous Intensity (candela, cd)
3. **Derived Quantities** — Formed from base quantities (speed = length/time, density = mass/volume)
4. **Scientific Notation** — Express very large/small numbers as `a × 10ⁿ` where 1 ≤ a < 10
5. **Prefixes** — kilo (k, 10³), mega (M, 10⁶), giga (G, 10⁹), milli (m, 10⁻³), micro (μ, 10⁻⁶), nano (n, 10⁻⁹)
6. **Significant Figures** — All non-zero digits count. Zeros between non-zero digits count. Leading zeros don't. Trailing zeros with decimal count.
7. **Measuring Instruments:**
   - Meter rule — 1 mm least count
   - Vernier caliper — 0.1 mm (0.01 cm) least count
   - Screw gauge — 0.01 mm (0.001 cm) least count
   - Stopwatch — 0.1 s or 0.01 s
   - Physical balance / Electronic balance
8. **Zero Error** — When an instrument shows non-zero reading with nothing measured. Must be added or subtracted.
9. **Precision & Accuracy** — Precision = closeness of repeated measurements. Accuracy = closeness to true value.

## Short Questions

**Q1. Define physical quantity with two examples.**

**Ans:** A physical quantity is a quantity that can be measured and has both magnitude and unit. For example, length (5 m) and mass (2 kg).

**Q2. What is the difference between base and derived quantities?**

**Ans:** 
- **Base quantities** are fundamental and cannot be derived from other quantities. There are 7 base quantities (length, mass, time, etc.).
- **Derived quantities** are formed by combining base quantities using mathematical operations. For example, speed (length/time), density (mass/volume).

**Q3. What are significant figures? Write rules for counting them.**

**Ans:** Significant figures are the reliable digits in a measurement that convey precision.
- All non-zero digits are significant
- Zeros between non-zero digits are significant
- Leading zeros are not significant
- Trailing zeros with decimal point are significant
- Trailing zeros without decimal point are not significant

**Q4. Define least count of an instrument. What is the least count of a meter rule?**

**Ans:** Least count is the smallest measurement that can be taken with an instrument. The least count of a meter rule is 1 mm (0.1 cm).

**Q5. Write SI units of mass, length, and time.**

**Ans:** Mass = kilogram (kg), Length = meter (m), Time = second (s).

**Q6. Convert 5 km into meters and express in scientific notation.**

**Ans:** 5 km = 5 × 10³ m = 5000 m. In scientific notation: 5 × 10³ m.

**Q7. What is zero error? How is it corrected?**

**Ans:** Zero error occurs when a measuring instrument shows a non-zero reading when it should show zero. If positive, subtract the error from the reading. If negative, add the absolute value.

**Q8. Differentiate between precision and accuracy.**

**Ans:** 
- **Precision** describes how close repeated measurements are to each other.
- **Accuracy** describes how close a measurement is to the true or accepted value.

**Q9. What are prefixes? Give three examples.**

**Ans:** Prefixes are symbols added before a unit to represent multiples or submultiples. Examples: kilo (×10³), milli (×10⁻³), micro (×10⁻⁶).

**Q10. Write the least count of vernier caliper and screw gauge.**

**Ans:** Vernier caliper = 0.1 mm (0.01 cm). Screw gauge = 0.01 mm (0.001 cm).

## Long Questions

**Q1. Explain the SI system of units. List the seven base quantities with their units and symbols.**

**Ans:** The International System of Units (SI) is the globally accepted system of measurement. It defines seven base quantities:

| Base Quantity | SI Unit | Symbol |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Length | meter | m |
| Mass | kilogram | kg |
| Time | second | s |
| Electric Current | ampere | A |
| Temperature | kelvin | K |
| Amount of Substance | mole | mol |
| Luminous Intensity | candela | cd |

All other units (newton, joule, watt, etc.) are derived from these seven base units.

**Q2. Describe how to measure the volume of a small irregular solid using a measuring cylinder.**

**Ans:** 
1. Fill a measuring cylinder with enough water to cover the solid. Record initial volume V₁.
2. Tie the solid with a thread and gently lower it into the water.
3. Record the new volume V₂ when the solid is fully submerged.
4. Volume of solid = V₂ − V₁
5. Repeat for accuracy and take average.

**Q3. Explain the construction and working of a vernier caliper.**

**Ans:** A vernier caliper consists of:
- Main scale with cm and mm markings
- Vernier scale that slides along the main scale with 10 divisions covering 9 mm (each vernier division = 0.9 mm)

**Least count** = Main scale division − Vernier scale division = 1 mm − 0.9 mm = 0.1 mm

**Measurement:** Main scale reading + (Vernier division coinciding × 0.1 mm)

Used to measure small lengths, diameters of objects, depths with moderate precision.

**Q4. Explain how a screw gauge works. Why is it more precise than a meter rule?**

**Ans:** A screw gauge has:
- A screw with fine threads (usually 0.5 mm or 1 mm pitch)
- A circular scale with 50 or 100 divisions
- Least count = Pitch / Number of circular divisions = 0.5 mm / 50 = 0.01 mm

It is more precise because:
- Screw mechanism magnifies small movements
- Circular scale provides finer reading than any linear scale
- Much smaller least count (0.01 mm vs 1 mm of meter rule)

**Q5. What are significant figures? Why are they important in measurements?**

**Ans:** Significant figures indicate the precision of a measurement.

**Rules:**
- 23.5 cm has 3 significant figures
- 0.0034 m has 2 significant figures
- 100.0 g has 4 significant figures
- 4500 m has 2 significant figures (unless decimal point is shown)

**Importance:** They tell us how reliable a measurement is. They prevent false precision (claiming more accuracy than actually measured) and help in proper rounding of calculations.

## Numerical Problems

**Q1. Convert the following:**
(a) 0.05 m into mm
(b) 2500 g into kg
(c) 72 km/h into m/s

**Solutions:**
(a) 0.05 × 1000 = 50 mm
(b) 2500 ÷ 1000 = 2.5 kg
(c) 72 × (1000/3600) = 72 × (5/18) = 20 m/s

**Q4. A screw gauge has a pitch of 0.5 mm and 50 circular divisions. What is its least count?**

**Solution:**
Least count = Pitch / Number of circular divisions
= 0.5 / 50 = 0.01 mm

---

**Q5. The diameter of a wire measured with a screw gauge is 2.34 mm. If the screw gauge has a zero error of +0.02 mm, what is the correct diameter?**

**Solution:**
Correct reading = Observed reading − Zero error
= 2.34 − (+0.02) = 2.34 − 0.02
= 2.32 mm

**Q6. A rectangular sheet measures 5.2 cm × 3.8 cm. Calculate its area with appropriate significant figures.**

**Solution:**
Area = 5.2 × 3.8 = 19.76 cm²

Since both measurements have 2 significant figures, the answer should have 2 significant figures.

Area = 20 cm²

---

**Q7. A car travels 150 m in 5.0 seconds. Find its average speed.**

**Solution:**
Speed = Distance / Time = 150 / 5.0 = 30 m/s

**Q8. The mass of a metal block is 245.6 g and its volume is 25.0 cm³. Calculate its density.**

**Solution:**
Density = Mass / Volume = 245.6 / 25.0 = 9.824 g/cm³

Mass has 4 significant figures, volume has 3. Answer should have 3.
Density = 9.82 g/cm³
