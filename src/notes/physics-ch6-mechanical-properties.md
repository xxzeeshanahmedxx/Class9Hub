# Chapter 6: Mechanical Properties of Matter

## Key Concepts

1. **Elasticity** — Property of a body to return to its original shape/size after deforming force is removed.
2. **Plasticity** — Property of a body to retain the deformed shape permanently after deforming force is removed.
3. **Stress** — Deforming force per unit area. σ = F/A (unit: Nm⁻² or Pa).
4. **Strain** — Ratio of change in dimension to original dimension (dimensionless). Three types: tensile, compressive, shear.
5. **Young's Modulus (Y)** — Ratio of tensile/compressive stress to longitudinal strain. Y = σ / ε = (F/A) / (ΔL/L).
6. **Hooke's Law** — Within elastic limit, extension in a spring is directly proportional to applied force. F = kx (k = spring constant).
7. **Spring Constant (k)** — Force per unit extension. k = F/x (unit: Nm⁻¹).
8. **Pressure** — Force acting normally per unit area. P = F/A (unit: Nm⁻² = Pa).
9. **Atmospheric Pressure** — Pressure exerted by the Earth's atmosphere. 1 atm = 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa.
10. **Density** — Mass per unit volume. ρ = m/V (unit: kgm⁻³).
11. **Archimedes Principle** — Upward buoyant force equals weight of fluid displaced.
12. **Principle of Floatation** — A floating body displaces its own weight of fluid.
13. **Pascal's Law** — Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally in all directions.
14. **Hydraulic Press** — Device based on Pascal's law. Magnifies force: F₂/F₁ = A₂/A₁.
15. **Viscosity** — Internal friction between fluid layers opposing relative motion.
16. **Terminal Velocity** — Constant velocity reached when drag force equals weight of falling object.
17. **Stokes Law** — Drag force on a sphere moving through a viscous fluid: F = 6πηrv.

## Short Questions

**Q1. Differentiate between elasticity and plasticity.**

**Ans:** Elasticity is the ability of a body to return to its original shape and size after the deforming force is removed (e.g., rubber band). Plasticity is the ability to retain the deformed shape permanently after the deforming force is removed (e.g., modeling clay).

**Q2. What is Hooke's law? Write its mathematical form.**

**Ans:** Hooke's law states that within the elastic limit, the extension produced in a spring is directly proportional to the applied force. Mathematically, F ∝ x or F = kx, where k is the spring constant.

**Q3. Define stress and strain. Give their SI units.**

**Ans:** Stress is the deforming force per unit area (σ = F/A). Its SI unit is Nm⁻² or Pascal (Pa). Strain is the ratio of change in dimension to the original dimension (ε = ΔL/L). It is dimensionless — no unit.

**Q4. What is Young's modulus? Write its formula.**

**Ans:** Young's modulus is the ratio of longitudinal stress to longitudinal strain within the elastic limit. Y = (F/A) / (ΔL/L) = FL / AΔL. Its SI unit is Nm⁻² (Pa).

**Q5. State Archimedes principle.**

**Ans:** Archimedes principle states that when a body is partially or completely immersed in a fluid, it experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the body.

**Q6. What is the principle of floatation?**

**Ans:** A floating body displaces its own weight of the fluid in which it floats. For an object to float, its density must be less than or equal to the density of the fluid.

**Q7. State Pascal's law. Give one application.**

**Ans:** Pascal's law states that pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished equally in all directions. Application: Hydraulic press (also hydraulic brakes, hydraulic lifts).

**Q8. What is viscosity? How does it change with temperature?**

**Ans:** Viscosity is the internal friction between adjacent layers of a fluid that opposes relative motion between them. Viscosity of liquids decreases with increase in temperature, while viscosity of gases increases with increase in temperature.

**Q9. Define terminal velocity.**

**Ans:** Terminal velocity is the constant maximum velocity attained by a falling object when the upward drag force (viscous resistance) balances the downward weight of the object, resulting in zero net acceleration.

**Q10. What is the difference between density and relative density?**

**Ans:** Density (ρ) is mass per unit volume of a substance (ρ = m/V), having SI unit kgm⁻³. Relative density (also called specific gravity) is the ratio of density of a substance to density of water at 4°C. It is dimensionless and has no unit.

## Long Questions

**Q1. Derive the expression for Young's modulus. Describe an experiment to determine it for a wire.**

**Ans:** Young's modulus (Y) is defined as the ratio of longitudinal stress to longitudinal strain within the elastic limit.

Consider a wire of length L and cross-sectional area A. A force F is applied along its length, causing an extension ΔL.

Stress = F/A
Strain = ΔL/L
Young's Modulus: Y = Stress / Strain = (F/A) / (ΔL/L) = FL / AΔL

**Experiment (Searle's apparatus):**
1. Take two identical long wires (usually steel) suspended from a rigid support.
2. Attach a main scale and vernier scale arrangement between the two wires.
3. The reference wire carries a fixed load; the experimental wire carries a load that can be varied.
4. Add weights to the experimental wire gradually and note the extension using the vernier scale.
5. Measure the length L with a meter scale, diameter d with a screw gauge (A = πd²/4).
6. Plot a stress-strain graph — it is a straight line up to the elastic limit.
7. Calculate Y from the slope of the graph using Y = FL / AΔL.

**Q2. State and explain Hooke's law. Draw the stress-strain graph and explain its different regions.**

**Ans:** Hooke's law: Within the elastic limit, stress is directly proportional to strain for a given material. σ ∝ ε or σ = Yε.

**Stress-Strain Graph:**
- **OA (Proportional limit):** Stress ∝ Strain, Hooke's law holds, graph is linear.
- **AB (Elastic region):** Graph deviates from linearity but body still returns to original shape when load is removed. Point B is the elastic limit.
- **BC (Plastic region):** Beyond elastic limit, permanent deformation occurs. The material yields without much increase in stress.
- **CD (Strain hardening):** Further stress increases strain at a slower rate due to rearrangement of internal structure.
- **Point D (Ultimate tensile strength):** Maximum stress the material can withstand.
- **DE (Necking):** Cross-section decreases locally, stress decreases, until fracture at point E (breaking point).

**Q3. Explain Pascal's law. Describe the working of a hydraulic press with a diagram.**

**Ans:** Pascal's law: Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted equally and undiminished to every part of the fluid and the walls of the container.

**Hydraulic Press Construction:**
A hydraulic press consists of two cylinders of different cross-sectional areas connected by a pipe filled with an incompressible fluid (usually oil). Each cylinder has a piston — a small piston of area A₁ and a large piston of area A₂.

**Working:**
1. A small force F₁ is applied on the small piston.
2. According to Pascal's law, the pressure P = F₁/A₁ is transmitted equally throughout the fluid.
3. The same pressure P acts on the large piston: P = F₂/A₂.
4. Therefore, F₂ = P × A₂ = (F₁/A₁) × A₂.
5. Since A₂ > A₁, the output force F₂ is magnified by the factor A₂/A₁.

Thus a small input force produces a large output force, making the hydraulic press a force-multiplying machine.

**Q4. Derive Stokes law and explain how terminal velocity is determined for a sphere falling through a viscous fluid.**

**Ans:** **Stokes Law:**
When a sphere of radius r moves through a viscous fluid of viscosity η with velocity v, the drag force F is given by:
F = 6πηrv

**Terminal Velocity Derivation:**
Consider a sphere of radius r and density ρ falling through a fluid of density σ.

Three forces act on the sphere:
1. **Weight** (downward): W = (4/3)πr³ρg
2. **Buoyant force** (upward): U = (4/3)πr³σg
3. **Viscous drag** (upward): F = 6πηrv

Initially, the sphere accelerates. As velocity increases, drag force increases. When net force becomes zero, the sphere falls with constant velocity (terminal velocity v_t).

At terminal velocity:
W - U - F = 0
(4/3)πr³ρg - (4/3)πr³σg - 6πηrv_t = 0
(4/3)πr³g(ρ - σ) = 6πηrv_t

Solving for v_t:
v_t = [2r²g(ρ - σ)] / 9η

This shows terminal velocity ∝ r², ∝ g, ∝ (ρ - σ), ∝ 1/η.

**Q5. Explain the principle of floatation with examples. Why does a ship made of iron float while an iron nail sinks?**

**Ans:** **Principle of Floatation:**
A floating body displaces its own weight of the fluid in which it floats. Mathematically, Weight of body = Weight of fluid displaced.

**Conditions for Floatation:**
1. If ρ_body < ρ_fluid: Body floats partially submerged.
2. If ρ_body = ρ_fluid: Body floats completely submerged at any depth.
3. If ρ_body > ρ_fluid: Body sinks.

**Why a ship floats but an iron nail sinks:**
A ship is made of iron (density ~7800 kgm⁻³, much denser than water ~1000 kgm⁻³). However, the ship is hollow and contains large air spaces. The average density of the ship (total mass / total volume including air spaces) becomes less than that of water. Therefore, it displaces enough water to equal its own weight and floats.

An iron nail is solid with no air spaces. Its density (7800 kgm⁻³) is greater than water (1000 kgm⁻³), so its weight exceeds the buoyant force, and it sinks.

## Numerical Problems

**Q1. A spring extends by 3 cm when a force of 6 N is applied. Find its spring constant and the extension for a force of 15 N.**

**Solution:**
Given: x₁ = 3 cm = 0.03 m, F₁ = 6 N, F₂ = 15 N

Spring constant: k = F₁/x₁ = 6/0.03 = 200 Nm⁻¹

For F₂ = 15 N: x₂ = F₂/k = 15/200 = 0.075 m = 7.5 cm

**Q2. A steel wire of length 2 m and cross-sectional area 1 × 10⁻⁶ m² stretches by 0.5 mm under a load of 100 N. Calculate Young's modulus of steel.**

**Solution:**
Given: L = 2 m, A = 1 × 10⁻⁶ m², ΔL = 0.5 × 10⁻³ m, F = 100 N

Stress = F/A = 100 / (1 × 10⁻⁶) = 1 × 10⁸ Pa
Strain = ΔL/L = 0.5 × 10⁻³ / 2 = 2.5 × 10⁻⁴

Y = Stress/Strain = 1 × 10⁸ / 2.5 × 10⁻⁴ = 4 × 10¹¹ Pa

**Q3. A force of 50 N acts normally on a surface of area 0.02 m². Calculate the pressure exerted.**

**Solution:**
Given: F = 50 N, A = 0.02 m²

P = F/A = 50/0.02 = 2500 Pa = 2.5 × 10³ Pa

**Q4. The small piston of a hydraulic press has an area of 0.005 m² and the large piston has an area of 0.1 m². If a force of 100 N is applied on the small piston, calculate the force on the large piston.**

**Solution:**
Given: A₁ = 0.005 m², A₂ = 0.1 m², F₁ = 100 N

Pressure P = F₁/A₁ = 100/0.005 = 20000 Pa

Force on large piston: F₂ = P × A₂ = 20000 × 0.1 = 2000 N

Alternatively, F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁) = 100 × (0.1/0.005) = 100 × 20 = 2000 N

**Q5. The density of gold is 19300 kgm⁻³ and volume of a gold bar is 0.0005 m³. Calculate its mass.**

**Solution:**
Given: ρ = 19300 kgm⁻³, V = 0.0005 m³

ρ = m/V ⇒ m = ρV = 19300 × 0.0005 = 9.65 kg

**Q6. A cube of side 0.1 m is completely immersed in water. Calculate the buoyant force acting on it. (Density of water = 1000 kgm⁻³, g = 10 ms⁻²)**

**Solution:**
Given: side = 0.1 m, ρ_water = 1000 kgm⁻³, g = 10 ms⁻²

Volume of cube = (0.1)³ = 0.001 m³

Weight of water displaced = ρVg = 1000 × 0.001 × 10 = 10 N

Buoyant force = Weight of water displaced = 10 N

**Q7. A steel ball of radius 2 mm falls through glycerine. Density of steel = 7800 kgm⁻³, density of glycerine = 1260 kgm⁻³, viscosity of glycerine = 0.83 Pas. Calculate the terminal velocity. (g = 10 ms⁻²)**

**Solution:**
Given: r = 2 × 10⁻³ m, ρ = 7800 kgm⁻³, σ = 1260 kgm⁻³, η = 0.83 Pas, g = 10 ms⁻²

v_t = 2r²g(ρ - σ) / 9η

v_t = 2 × (2 × 10⁻³)² × 10 × (7800 - 1260) / (9 × 0.83)

v_t = 2 × 4 × 10⁻⁶ × 10 × 6540 / (9 × 0.83)

v_t = 2 × 4 × 10⁻⁶ × 10 × 6540 / 7.47

v_t = 0.5232 / 7.47 = 0.07 ms⁻¹ (approximately)

**Q8. A spring of spring constant 500 Nm⁻¹ is compressed by 0.02 m. Calculate the force stored in the spring and the work done.**

**Solution:**
Given: k = 500 Nm⁻¹, x = 0.02 m

Force: F = kx = 500 × 0.02 = 10 N

Work done = Elastic potential energy = (1/2)kx² = 0.5 × 500 × (0.02)²

= 0.5 × 500 × 0.0004 = 0.5 × 0.2 = 0.1 J
