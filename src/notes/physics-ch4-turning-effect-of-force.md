# Chapter 4: Turning Effect of Force

## Key Concepts

1. **Like Parallel Forces** — Forces acting in the same direction along parallel lines. Their resultant equals the sum of the forces and acts parallel to them in the same direction.

2. **Unlike Parallel Forces** — Forces acting in opposite directions along parallel lines. Their resultant equals the difference of the forces and acts in the direction of the larger force.

3. **Resultant of Forces** — A single force that produces the same effect as two or more forces acting together. For parallel forces, magnitude is the algebraic sum; for non-parallel forces, it follows the parallelogram law.

4. **Torque or Moment of Force (τ)** — The turning effect of a force about an axis. τ = F × d, where F is force and d is perpendicular distance (moment arm). SI unit: N m.

5. **Clockwise and Anti-Clockwise Moments** — A moment that rotates an object clockwise is a clockwise moment; anti-clockwise similarly. Convention: anti-clockwise is positive.

6. **Principle of Moments** — For a body in equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of anti-clockwise moments about the same point. Στ_clockwise = Στ_anti-clockwise.

7. **Center of Mass** — The point where the entire mass of a body is considered to be concentrated. For symmetrical bodies, it lies at the geometric center.

8. **Center of Gravity** — The point where the entire weight of a body acts. For uniform gravitational fields, it coincides with the center of mass.

9. **Equilibrium** — A body is in equilibrium when the net force and net torque acting on it are both zero.

10. **First Condition of Equilibrium** — The vector sum of all forces acting on a body is zero. ΣF = 0 (ΣF_x = 0, ΣF_y = 0). Ensures no translational acceleration.

11. **Second Condition of Equilibrium** — The sum of all torques acting on a body about any point is zero. Στ = 0. Ensures no rotational acceleration.

12. **Stable Equilibrium** — Body returns to original position after a slight displacement. Center of gravity is lowest. Example: a ball in a bowl.

13. **Unstable Equilibrium** — Body moves further away from original position after a slight displacement. Center of gravity is highest. Example: a ball on a hilltop.

14. **Neutral Equilibrium** — Body stays in new position after displacement. Center of gravity remains at same height. Example: a ball on a flat surface.

15. **Stability** — The resistance of a body to toppling. Increased by: lowering center of gravity, widening the base, increasing mass.

## Short Questions

**Q1. Define torque or moment of force. Write its formula and SI unit.**

**Ans:** Torque is the turning effect produced by a force acting on a body about an axis. It is given by τ = F × d, where F is the force and d is the perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action of the force. Its SI unit is newton-meter (N m).

**Q2. Differentiate between like and unlike parallel forces.**

**Ans:** 
- **Like parallel forces** act in the same direction along parallel lines. Their resultant equals the sum of the forces. Example: two people pushing a car forward.
- **Unlike parallel forces** act in opposite directions along parallel lines. Their resultant equals the difference of the forces. Example: two people pushing a car in opposite directions.

**Q3. State the principle of moments.**

**Ans:** According to the principle of moments, when a body is in equilibrium under the action of forces, the sum of clockwise moments about any point is equal to the sum of anti-clockwise moments about the same point. Mathematically: Στ_clockwise = Στ_anti-clockwise.

**Q4. What is center of gravity? How is it related to center of mass?**

**Ans:** The center of gravity is the point where the entire weight of a body acts. The center of mass is the point where the entire mass is concentrated. In a uniform gravitational field, the center of gravity coincides with the center of mass.

**Q5. State the first condition of equilibrium.**

**Ans:** The first condition of equilibrium states that the vector sum of all forces acting on a body must be zero. Mathematically: ΣF = 0, which gives ΣF_x = 0 and ΣF_y = 0. This ensures the body has no translational acceleration.

**Q6. State the second condition of equilibrium.**

**Ans:** The second condition of equilibrium states that the sum of all torques acting on a body about any point must be zero. Mathematically: Στ = 0. This ensures the body has no rotational acceleration.

**Q7. What makes a body more stable against toppling?**

**Ans:** A body is more stable if it has: (i) a lower center of gravity, (ii) a wider base, and (iii) greater mass. These factors increase the torque required to topple it.

**Q8. Differentiate between stable and unstable equilibrium.**

**Ans:** 
- **Stable equilibrium:** When displaced slightly, the body returns to its original position. The center of gravity is at the lowest point.
- **Unstable equilibrium:** When displaced slightly, the body moves further away from its original position. The center of gravity is at the highest point.

**Q9. Why is a longer spanner preferred to loosen a tight nut?**

**Ans:** Torque = Force × Moment arm. A longer spanner provides a larger moment arm (perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of force). For the same applied force, a larger moment arm produces greater torque, making it easier to loosen the nut.

**Q10. Give an example of neutral equilibrium from daily life.**

**Ans:** A ball lying on a flat horizontal surface is in neutral equilibrium. When pushed, it moves to a new position and stays there without returning or moving further away. The center of gravity remains at the same height throughout.

## Long Questions

**Q1. Explain the concept of torque in detail. How does the moment arm affect the turning effect? Give examples.**

**Ans:** Torque (or moment of force) is the turning effect produced when a force acts on a body at some distance from its axis of rotation. It is a vector quantity given by:

τ = F × d

where F is the applied force and d is the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the line of action of the force (called the moment arm).

The turning effect depends on:
1. **Magnitude of force** — Larger force produces greater torque.
2. **Length of moment arm** — For the same force, a larger moment arm gives greater torque.

**Examples:**
- Opening a door: Pushing at the handle (far from hinges) requires less force than pushing near the hinges.
- Spanner: A longer spanner creates more torque for the same applied force.
- Seesaw: A lighter person can balance a heavier person by sitting farther from the pivot.

If the force acts directly through the pivot (d = 0), torque is zero and no turning occurs.

**Q2. State and explain the two conditions of equilibrium with examples.**

**Ans:** For a body to be in complete equilibrium, both conditions must be satisfied:

**First Condition of Equilibrium (Translational Equilibrium):**
The vector sum of all forces acting on the body is zero.
ΣF = 0 → ΣF_x = 0 and ΣF_y = 0
This ensures the body does not accelerate linearly. If the net force is zero, the body is either at rest or moving with constant velocity.

**Example:** A book resting on a table. Weight downward is balanced by the normal reaction force upward. ΣF_y = 0.

**Second Condition of Equilibrium (Rotational Equilibrium):**
The sum of all torques acting on the body about any point is zero.
Στ = 0
This ensures the body does not rotate.

**Example:** A balanced seesaw. The clockwise torque of one person equals the anti-clockwise torque of the other person.

A book on a table satisfies both conditions: it is not moving translationally (ΣF = 0) and not rotating (Στ = 0).

**Q3. Explain the principle of moments. How can it be verified experimentally?**

**Ans:** The principle of moments states that for a body in equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of anti-clockwise moments about the same point.

**Experimental Verification:**
1. Take a meter rule and balance it at its center (50 cm mark) on a knife edge.
2. Hang a known weight W₁ on the left side at a distance d₁ from the pivot.
3. Hang another known weight W₂ on the right side and adjust its position until the rule balances horizontally.
4. Measure the distance d₂ from the pivot to W₂.
5. Calculate the anti-clockwise moment = W₁ × d₁ (if W₁ is on the left).
6. Calculate the clockwise moment = W₂ × d₂.
7. It will be found that W₁ × d₁ = W₂ × d₂, verifying the principle.

The principle is widely applied in levers, balances, seesaws, and all systems involving rotational equilibrium.

**Q4. What is center of mass and center of gravity? How does the position of center of gravity affect stability?**

**Ans:** 

**Center of Mass:** The point at which the entire mass of a body is considered to be concentrated. For a symmetrical body of uniform density, it lies at its geometric center.

**Center of Gravity:** The point at which the entire weight of a body acts. In a uniform gravitational field, it coincides with the center of mass.

**Effect of Center of Gravity on Stability:**
- A body with a **low center of gravity** is more stable because a larger torque is required to topple it. The body returns to its original position after displacement.
- A body with a **high center of gravity** is less stable because even a small displacement can cause it to topple.

**Examples:**
- Racing cars have a low center of gravity to prevent rolling over.
- Tall buildings have wide bases to keep the center of gravity within the base.
- A person carrying a heavy load on the head walks upright to keep the center of gravity aligned.
- A double-decker bus is less stable than a regular bus because its center of gravity is higher.

**Q5. Describe the three types of equilibrium with examples from daily life.**

**Ans:** 

**1. Stable Equilibrium:**
- When displaced, the body returns to its original position.
- Center of gravity is at the lowest possible position.
- Example: A pendulum at rest, a ball in a concave bowl, a cone resting on its base.
- When displaced, the center of gravity rises, and gravity pulls it back down to the lowest point.

**2. Unstable Equilibrium:**
- When displaced, the body moves further away from its original position.
- Center of gravity is at the highest possible position.
- Example: A pencil balanced on its tip, a cone balanced on its vertex, a ball on a convex surface.
- The slightest disturbance causes the center of gravity to move lower, taking the body further from equilibrium.

**3. Neutral Equilibrium:**
- When displaced, the body stays in its new position.
- Center of gravity remains at the same height regardless of displacement.
- Example: A ball on a flat horizontal surface, a cone lying on its side, a rolling cylinder.
- The center of gravity neither rises nor falls during displacement.

## Numerical Problems

**Q1. A force of 50 N is applied at the end of a spanner of length 0.2 m. Calculate the torque produced.**

**Solution:**
τ = F × d
τ = 50 × 0.2
τ = 10 N m

---

**Q2. Two like parallel forces of 30 N and 50 N act on a body. Find the magnitude and direction of their resultant.**

**Solution:**
Since forces are like (same direction):
R = F₁ + F₂
R = 30 + 50
R = 80 N
Direction: Same as the individual forces.

---

**Q3. Two unlike parallel forces of 60 N and 25 N act on a body in opposite directions. Find the resultant.**

**Solution:**
Since forces are unlike (opposite directions):
R = F₁ − F₂ (larger − smaller)
R = 60 − 25
R = 35 N
Direction: In the direction of the larger force (60 N).

---

**Q4. A force of 20 N is applied at a distance of 0.5 m from the pivot. If the moment arm is increased to 0.8 m, what will be the new torque for the same force?**

**Solution:**
Initial torque: τ₁ = 20 × 0.5 = 10 N m
New torque: τ₂ = 20 × 0.8 = 16 N m
Increase in torque = 16 − 10 = 6 N m

---

**Q5. A see-saw is balanced with a 40 kg child sitting 1.5 m from the pivot. Where should a 30 kg child sit to balance it? (Take g = 10 m/s²)**

**Solution:**
Weight of first child = 40 × 10 = 400 N
Weight of second child = 30 × 10 = 300 N

By principle of moments:
Clockwise moment = Anti-clockwise moment
400 × 1.5 = 300 × d₂
600 = 300 × d₂
d₂ = 600 / 300
d₂ = 2 m

The 30 kg child should sit 2 m from the pivot on the opposite side.

---

**Q6. A uniform meter rule of weight 1.5 N is balanced at its center. A weight of 2 N is suspended at the 20 cm mark. Where must a weight of 3 N be suspended to balance the rule?**

**Solution:**
The meter rule is uniformly balanced at 50 cm (center).
2 N weight at 20 cm mark.

Distance of 2 N weight from center = 50 − 20 = 30 cm = 0.3 m
Moment due to 2 N = 2 × 0.3 = 0.6 N m (clockwise)

Let the 3 N weight be placed at distance d from center on the other side.
Anti-clockwise moment = 3 × d

For equilibrium: 3 × d = 0.6
d = 0.6 / 3 = 0.2 m = 20 cm

The 3 N weight must be hung at 50 + 20 = 70 cm mark.

---

**Q7. A mechanic applies a force of 150 N to a wrench of length 0.3 m to tighten a bolt. What torque does he apply? If he uses a wrench of length 0.5 m with the same force, what will be the new torque?**

**Solution:**
τ₁ = F × d₁ = 150 × 0.3 = 45 N m
τ₂ = F × d₂ = 150 × 0.5 = 75 N m

---

**Q8. A uniform rod of weight 12 N is 2 m long. It is pivoted at its center. A weight of 8 N is hung at one end. What weight must be hung at the other end to balance the rod?**

**Solution:**
The rod is balanced at its center (1 m from each end).
8 N weight at one end: distance from pivot = 1 m
Moment = 8 × 1 = 8 N m

Let required weight = W at distance 1 m on the other side.
W × 1 = 8
W = 8 N

A weight of 8 N must be hung at the other end.
