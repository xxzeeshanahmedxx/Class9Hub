# Chapter 8: Magnetism

## Key Concepts

1. A magnet is an object that produces a magnetic field and attracts ferromagnetic materials like iron, nickel, and cobalt.
2. Every magnet has two poles: North Pole and South Pole. Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.
3. Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines that represent the direction and strength of a magnetic field. They run from North to South outside the magnet.
4. Magnetic materials can be classified as ferromagnetic (strongly attracted), paramagnetic (weakly attracted), and diamagnetic (weakly repelled).
5. Induced magnetism occurs when a magnetic material becomes magnetized when placed near a magnet.
6. Electromagnetism is the production of a magnetic field by an electric current flowing through a conductor.
7. A current-carrying conductor produces a circular magnetic field around it. The direction is given by the Right-Hand Rule.
8. An electromagnet is a coil of wire wound around a soft iron core that becomes magnetic only when current flows through it.
9. The strength of an electromagnet depends on the number of turns, current magnitude, and the type of core material.
10. An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy using the interaction between magnetic fields and current-carrying conductors.
11. An electric generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy using electromagnetic induction.
12. A magnetic compass uses a freely rotating magnetized needle to align with the Earth's magnetic field.
13. The Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet with its magnetic South Pole near the geographic North Pole.
14. Magnets are used in speakers, hard drives, MRI machines, electric motors, generators, and magnetic separators.
15. The magnetic field is strongest at the poles and weakest at the center of a magnet.

## Short Questions

**Q1. Define a magnet and list its types.**

**Ans:** A magnet is an object that produces a magnetic field and attracts ferromagnetic materials. There are two types: (i) Natural magnets — found in nature, e.g., lodestone (magnetite). (ii) Artificial magnets — man-made, e.g., bar magnet, horseshoe magnet, electromagnet.

**Q2. What is the difference between ferromagnetic and diamagnetic materials?**

**Ans:** Ferromagnetic materials are strongly attracted to magnets and can be magnetized (e.g., iron, nickel, cobalt). Diamagnetic materials are weakly repelled by magnets and cannot be permanently magnetized (e.g., copper, bismuth, water).

**Q3. What are magnetic field lines? State any two properties.**

**Ans:** Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines used to represent the magnetic field around a magnet. Properties: (i) They start from the North Pole and end at the South Pole outside the magnet. (ii) They never intersect each other.

**Q4. Explain induced magnetism with an example.**

**Ans:** Induced magnetism is the process by which a magnetic material becomes temporarily magnetized when placed near a magnet. Example: When a soft iron nail is brought near a bar magnet, the nail attracts other tiny iron pins — it becomes an induced magnet.

**Q5. State the Right-Hand Thumb Rule.**

**Ans:** The Right-Hand Thumb Rule states: If a current-carrying conductor is held in the right hand such that the thumb points in the direction of current, then the curled fingers give the direction of the magnetic field lines around the conductor.

**Q6. What is an electromagnet? How can its strength be increased?**

**Ans:** An electromagnet is a coil of wire wound around a soft iron core that behaves like a magnet only when current passes through it. Its strength can be increased by: (i) increasing the number of turns of the coil, (ii) increasing the current, (iii) using a soft iron core instead of air.

**Q7. What is the principle of an electric motor?**

**Ans:** An electric motor works on the principle that a current-carrying conductor placed in a magnetic field experiences a force (mechanical force). This force causes the coil to rotate, converting electrical energy into mechanical energy.

**Q8. Distinguish between an electric motor and an electric generator.**

**Ans:** An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy and works on the magnetic effect of current. An electric generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy and works on the principle of electromagnetic induction.

**Q9. Why does a magnetic compass needle point north-south?**

**Ans:** The Earth itself acts like a giant bar magnet with its magnetic South Pole near the geographic North Pole. The compass needle (a small magnet) aligns itself with the Earth's magnetic field, so its North Pole points towards the geographic North Pole.

**Q10. Write any three practical uses of magnets in daily life.**

**Ans:** (i) In loudspeakers and headphones to convert electrical signals into sound. (ii) In hard disk drives to store data magnetically. (iii) In MRI machines to produce detailed images of internal body organs.

## Long Questions

**Q1. Describe in detail the properties of magnets and the characteristics of magnetic field lines.**

**Ans:** Magnets have the following properties:
- **Attractive property:** Magnets attract ferromagnetic materials such as iron, nickel, and cobalt.
- **Directive property:** A freely suspended magnet always aligns itself in the north-south direction.
- **Polar property:** Every magnet has two poles — North and South. Like poles repel, unlike poles attract.
- **Inductive property:** A magnet can induce magnetism in nearby magnetic materials.
- **Pair property:** Poles always exist in pairs. If a magnet is broken, each piece becomes a complete magnet with both poles.

**Magnetic field lines** have these characteristics:
- They emerge from the North Pole and enter the South Pole outside the magnet. Inside the magnet, they run from South to North, forming closed loops.
- The tangent at any point on a field line gives the direction of the magnetic field at that point.
- They never intersect each other. If they did, it would mean two directions of the field at the same point, which is impossible.
- The density of field lines indicates the strength of the magnetic field — closer lines mean a stronger field.
- The field is strongest near the poles where the lines are most concentrated.

**Q2. Explain the phenomenon of electromagnetism. Describe the magnetic field pattern around a straight conductor and a solenoid.**

**Ans:** Electromagnetism is the production of a magnetic field by an electric current. In 1820, Hans Christian Oersted discovered that a current-carrying wire deflects a nearby compass needle, proving that electricity and magnetism are related.

**Magnetic field around a straight conductor:** When current flows through a straight conductor, concentric circular magnetic field lines are produced around it. Using the Right-Hand Thumb Rule, if the thumb points in the direction of current, the curled fingers show the direction of the magnetic field. The field strength decreases as the distance from the conductor increases.

**Magnetic field of a solenoid:** A solenoid is a long coil of wire with many turns. When current passes through it, the magnetic field inside the solenoid is strong and uniform (parallel and equally spaced). The field outside the solenoid is weak and similar to that of a bar magnet. One end of the solenoid acts as North Pole and the other as South Pole.

**Q3. Describe the construction, working, and applications of an electromagnet.**

**Ans:** An electromagnet is a temporary magnet made by winding a coil of insulated copper wire around a soft iron core.

**Construction:** A soft iron rod (core) is taken. An insulated copper wire is wound tightly around it in the form of a coil. The two ends of the wire are connected to a battery through a switch.

**Working:** When current flows through the coil, the soft iron core becomes magnetized and attracts magnetic materials. When the current is switched off, the core loses its magnetism almost completely.

**Applications:**
- **Electric bells:** The electromagnet attracts the armature, causing the hammer to strike the gong.
- **Cranes:** Large electromagnets lift heavy scrap iron and steel in junkyards.
- **Magnetic separators:** Separate iron ore from other materials.
- **Relays and switches:** Used in circuits to control high-power devices with small control signals.
- **MRI machines:** Powerful electromagnets produce strong magnetic fields for medical imaging.
- **Loudspeakers:** Electromagnets vibrate the diaphragm to produce sound.

**Q4. Explain the working principle of an electric generator with the help of a labeled diagram.**

**Ans:** An electric generator converts mechanical energy into electrical energy based on electromagnetic induction, discovered by Michael Faraday. When a conductor moves in a magnetic field, an emf (electromotive force) is induced across it.

**Construction:** A rectangular coil of copper wire is placed between the poles of a strong horseshoe magnet. The coil is mounted on an axle and can be rotated mechanically. The ends of the coil are connected to slip rings (for AC) or a split-ring commutator (for DC). Carbon brushes make contact with the rings to transfer the induced current to the external circuit.

**Working:** When the coil is rotated mechanically in the magnetic field, the magnetic flux through the coil changes continuously. This change in flux induces an emf in the coil according to Faraday's law. The induced current flows through the external circuit. In an AC generator, the current direction reverses each half-cycle. In a DC generator, a commutator ensures current flows in one direction.

**Importance:** Generators are the primary source of electricity in power stations, using turbines driven by water, steam, or wind to rotate the coil.

**Q5. Discuss the Earth's magnetic field, the working of a magnetic compass, and the practical significance of magnetism.**

**Ans:** The Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet with its magnetic field extending far into space. The magnetic South Pole is located near the geographic North Pole (approximately 11.5 degrees off the rotational axis). The source of the Earth's magnetism is believed to be the motion of molten iron and nickel in the outer core, generating electric currents that produce the magnetic field.

**Magnetic compass:** A compass consists of a small, lightweight magnetized needle freely pivoted on a frictionless bearing. The needle aligns itself with the Earth's magnetic field, with its North Pole pointing towards the geographic North Pole (magnetic south). The compass is essential for navigation and has been used for centuries by travelers, explorers, and sailors.

**Practical significance:**
- **Navigation:** Compasses enable direction-finding on land, sea, and air.
- **Protection from solar wind:** The Earth's magnetic field deflects charged particles from the Sun, protecting the atmosphere and life.
- **Technology:** Magnets are essential in motors, generators, transformers, speakers, hard drives, credit cards, and magnetic resonance imaging (MRI).
- **Scientific research:** Magnetism is used in particle accelerators (e.g., CERN's Large Hadron Collider) and in studying the structure of materials.

## Numerical Problems

**Q1. A straight conductor carries a current of 5 A. Calculate the magnetic field strength at a distance of 0.1 m from the conductor. (Take \u00b5\u2080 = 4\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077 Tm/A)**

**Solution:**
The magnetic field due to a long straight conductor is:
B = (\u00b5\u2080 I) / (2\u03c0 r)

B = (4\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077 \u00d7 5) / (2\u03c0 \u00d7 0.1)
B = (20\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077) / (0.2\u03c0)
B = 100 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077
B = 1.0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2075 T

Thus, the magnetic field strength is 1.0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2075 Tesla.

**Q2. A current of 2 A flows through a solenoid having 500 turns and length 0.25 m. Find the magnetic field inside the solenoid.**

**Solution:**
For a solenoid, B = \u00b5\u2080 n I
where n = number of turns per unit length = N/L

n = 500 / 0.25 = 2000 turns/m

B = (4\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077) \u00d7 2000 \u00d7 2
B = 4\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077 \u00d7 4000
B = 16\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2074
B = 5.03 \u00d7 10\u207b\u00b3 T

Thus, the magnetic field inside the solenoid is approximately 5.03 \u00d7 10\u207b\u00b3 T.

**Q3. A force of 0.5 N acts on a conductor of length 0.2 m placed perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.4 T. Calculate the current flowing through the conductor.**

**Solution:**
The force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field is:
F = B I L sin\u03b8

For \u03b8 = 90\u00b0, sin 90\u00b0 = 1
F = B I L

Rearranging:
I = F / (B L)
I = 0.5 / (0.4 \u00d7 0.2)
I = 0.5 / 0.08
I = 6.25 A

Thus, the current flowing is 6.25 A.

**Q4. A magnetic pole of strength 20 A-m is placed in a uniform magnetic field of 0.3 T. Calculate the force experienced by the pole.**

**Solution:**
Force on a magnetic pole: F = m B
where m = pole strength, B = magnetic field

F = 20 \u00d7 0.3
F = 6.0 N

Thus, the force experienced is 6.0 N.

**Q5. A circular coil of radius 0.05 m has 100 turns and carries a current of 3 A. Find the magnetic field at the center of the coil.**

**Solution:**
The magnetic field at the center of a circular coil is:
B = (\u00b5\u2080 N I) / (2r)

B = (4\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077 \u00d7 100 \u00d7 3) / (2 \u00d7 0.05)
B = (4\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077 \u00d7 300) / 0.1
B = (1200\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077) / 0.1
B = 12000\u03c0 \u00d7 10\u207b\u2077
B = 3.77 \u00d7 10\u207b\u00b3 T

Thus, the magnetic field at the center is approximately 3.77 \u00d7 10\u207b\u00b3 T.

**Q6. A wire of length 0.5 m moves with a velocity of 10 m/s perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.2 T. Calculate the induced emf.**

**Solution:**
The induced emf in a moving conductor is:
\u03b5 = B L v

\u03b5 = 0.2 \u00d7 0.5 \u00d7 10
\u03b5 = 1.0 V

Thus, the induced emf is 1.0 V.

**Q7. The magnetic flux through a coil of 100 turns changes from 0.05 Wb to 0.01 Wb in 0.2 s. Calculate the average induced emf.**

**Solution:**
Using Faraday's law:
\u03b5 = -N (\u0394\u03a6 / \u0394t)

\u0394\u03a6 = \u03a6\u2082 - \u03a6\u2081 = 0.01 - 0.05 = -0.04 Wb
\u0394t = 0.2 s

\u03b5 = -100 \u00d7 (-0.04 / 0.2)
\u03b5 = 100 \u00d7 0.2
\u03b5 = 20 V

Thus, the average induced emf is 20 V.
