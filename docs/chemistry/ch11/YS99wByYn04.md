# Chemical Reactions of Alkanes — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This lesson explores the reactivity of Alkanes. Since alkanes are "paraffins" (low reactivity), the teacher focuses on the few, highly important reactions they do undergo: Combustion and Halogenation.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Our teacher tells us that while alkanes don't react with acids or bases, they burn excellently. Complete Combustion of alkanes (in plenty of oxygen) produces Carbon Dioxide, Water, and massive amounts of heat. The teacher then details Halogenation—specifically substitution reactions where chlorine atoms replace hydrogen atoms one by one in the presence of diffused sunlight.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Combustion:** Burning a hydrocarbon in the presence of oxygen to produce $CO_2$, $H_2O$, and heat.
- **Substitution Reaction:** A reaction where one atom in a molecule is replaced by another atom (e.g., Cl replacing H).
- **Halogenation:** The substitution of hydrogen atoms in an alkane by halogen atoms.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- Methane Combustion: $CH_4 + 2O_2 
ightarrow CO_2 + 2H_2O + 	ext{Heat}$.
- Incomplete combustion (low oxygen) produces toxic Carbon Monoxide (CO) and soot (Carbon).
- Chlorination of methane happens in steps: forming Chloromethane, Dichloromethane, Chloroform, and finally Carbon Tetrachloride.

## What Students Should Remember
Honestly, the distinction between complete and incomplete combustion is a board exam favorite. You must emphasize that lack of oxygen leads to the deadly production of Carbon Monoxide (CO) instead of the safe Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$).
