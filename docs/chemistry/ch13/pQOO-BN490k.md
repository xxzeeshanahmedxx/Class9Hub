# Hazard Signs to Emergency Situation in the Lab — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This practical safety lecture teaches students how to read the visual language of the laboratory. The teacher covers international Hazard Signs and the strict protocols required to handle Emergency Situations.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Our teacher displays the standard warning pictograms found on reagent bottles. The teacher explains the symbols for Flammable (a flame), Toxic (a skull and crossbones), Corrosive (acid eating through a hand/metal), and Oxidizing (a flame over a circle). The lecture then transitions into emergency management, detailing what to do if a fire breaks out or glass breaks.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Toxic:** A substance that can cause death or serious injury if swallowed, inhaled, or absorbed through the skin.
- **Corrosive:** A highly reactive substance that causes obvious damage to living tissue (skin) or metal upon contact.
- **Flammable:** A chemical that catches fire easily at normal temperatures.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- **Flammable liquids** (like alcohol) must be kept far away from open Bunsen burner flames.
- **Toxic gases** must only be used inside a ventilated Fume Hood.
- In case of a fire, immediately turn off the main gas valve and alert the teacher.
- Never pick up broken glass with bare hands; use a brush and dustpan.

## What Students Should Remember
Look, memorizing the shapes of the hazard symbols is mandatory for MCQs. The difference between the Flammable symbol (just fire) and the Oxidizing symbol (fire over an 'O') is subtle but extremely important, as oxidizers can cause other materials to spontaneously combust.
