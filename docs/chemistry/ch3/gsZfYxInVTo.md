# Electropositive Character of Metals and Non-Metals — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This theoretical lecture compares the fundamental chemical nature of Metals versus Non-Metals. The teacher focuses on "Electropositivity" as the defining characteristic that separates the two sides of the periodic table.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The teacher defines Electropositivity as the tendency of an atom to lose electrons and form positive ions (cations). Metals (like Sodium and Magnesium) are highly electropositive because they have 1, 2, or 3 valence electrons and low ionization energy. Non-metals (like Oxygen and Chlorine) are highly electronegative because they have 5, 6, or 7 valence electrons and prefer to *gain* electrons to complete their octet.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Electropositivity (Metallic Character):** The ability of an element to easily lose valence electrons to form cations.
- **Electronegativity (Non-Metallic Character):** The ability of an element to attract and gain electrons to form anions.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- **Metals:** High electropositivity, low ionization energy, form basic oxides, solid at room temp (except mercury).
- **Non-Metals:** High electronegativity, high ionization energy, form acidic oxides, exist as gases/liquids/brittle solids.
- Electropositivity increases as you go *down* a group because atomic size increases, making it easier to lose the outermost electron.

## What Students Should Remember
Listen carefully here: Make sure you understand the trend! a frequent mcq asks which metal is more reactive. because electropositivity increases down a group, potassium (k) is more electropositive and reacts more violently than sodium (na), even though they are in the same column.
