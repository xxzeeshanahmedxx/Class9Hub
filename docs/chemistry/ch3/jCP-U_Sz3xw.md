# Compare of Ionic and Covalent Compounds — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This is a high-yield exam preparation lecture. The teacher directly contrasts the macroscopic physical properties of Ionic Compounds (like salt) versus Covalent Compounds (like wax or water), linking their properties back to their microscopic bond types.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Sir creates a comparative analysis. Ionic compounds form strong, rigid 3D crystal lattices, leading to very high melting and boiling points. They conduct electricity, but ONLY when melted or dissolved in water (because the ions become free to move). Covalent compounds form distinct, individual molecules held together by weak intermolecular forces. Therefore, they have low melting points, are often gases or liquids, and never conduct electricity because they have no free ions.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Crystal Lattice:** The symmetrical 3D arrangement of atoms/ions inside a solid.
- **Aqueous Solution:** A solution where the solvent is water (the state required for ionic compounds to conduct electricity).

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
| Property | Ionic Compounds (e.g., NaCl) | Covalent Compounds (e.g., $CH_4$, $H_2O$) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **State** | Hard, crystalline solids | Gases, liquids, or soft solids |
| **Melting/Boiling Points** | Very High | Very Low |
| **Solubility** | Soluble in water (polar) | Soluble in organic solvents (non-polar) |
| **Electrical Conductivity** | Good conductors (when molten/aqueous) | Poor/Non-conductors |

## What Students Should Remember
Pro tip: The conductivity rule is the most common trick question. solid table salt ($nacl$) does not conduct electricity because the ions are locked tightly in the lattice. it must be dissolved in water (aqueous) or melted to conduct!
