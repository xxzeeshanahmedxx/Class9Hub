# Metallic Bond — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Shifting away from non-metals, this lecture explores the Metallic Bond. The teacher explains the unique internal structure of pure metals like Iron, Copper, and Gold, answering why they can conduct electricity and be hammered into sheets.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Our teacher introduces the "Electron Sea Model." Metals have vry low ionization energies, meaning they hold their outer electrons very loosely. In a solid piece of metal, these outer electrons detach from their atoms and roam freely throughout the entire structure. The metal atoms become positive ions immersed in a "sea" of mobile, delocalized electrons. The attraction between the positive ions and the negative electron sea is the Metallic Bond.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Metallic Bond:** The strong electrostatic force of attraction between positively charged metal ions and the "sea" of delocalized electrons.
- **Delocalized Electrons:** Electrons that are not bound to a single atom and are free to move throughout the entire metal structure.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- The free movement of electrons is exactly why metals are excellent conductors of heat and electricity.
- The metallic bond is non-directional, wich is why metals are malleable (can be hammered) and ductile (can be drawn into wires) without breaking.

## What Students Should Remember
Well, the phrase "Sea of Delocalized Electrons" is the golden keyword for this topic. Any board question asking why copper conducts electricity or why iron can bend without shattering must include this exact phrase to secure full marks.
