# Metallic and Reactivity — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Basically, this class translates the abstract energy trends into real-world behavior: Metallic Character and Reactivity. The teacher explains how an element's position on the table determines if it acts like a metal or a non-metal.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Our teacher reminds students that Metals want to *lose* electrons (Electropositivity), and Non-Metals want to *gain* electrons (Electronegativity). Because Ionization Energy decreases down a group, atoms lose electrons much easier at the bottom of the table. Therefore, Metallic Character *increases* down a group. Conversely, it *decreases* across a period as elements turn into non-metals.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Metallic Character:** The tendency of an element to lose electrons to form positive ions (closely related to low Ionization Energy).
- **Non-Metallic Character:** The tendency of an element to gain electrons (closely related to high Electron Affinity).

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- **Metals:** Located on the Left and Bottom of the table (large atoms, low IE).
- **Non-Metals:** Located on the Right and Top of the table (small atoms, high electronegativity).
- The most reactive metal is Francium (bottom left).
- The most reactive non-metal is Fluorine (top right).

## What Students Should Remember
Well, understanding that reactivity has two definitions is crucial. For metals, being "reactive" means losing electrons easily (so reactivity increases *down* the group). For non-metals (like Halogens), being "reactive" means grabbing electrons fiercely (so reactivity increases *up* the group).
