# Chemical Formula of Compounds — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Continuing from binary compounds, this lecture tackles more complex chemical formulas. The teacher explains how to write the formulas for compounds containing Polyatomic Ions (radicals).

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Sir defines Polyatomic Ions as groups of covalently bonded atoms that carry an overall net electrical charge, acting as a single unit (like Sulfate $SO_4^{-2}$ or Hydroxide $OH^-$). The teacher demonstrates how to apply the criss-cross method to these radicals. Crucially, if the subscript crossing over is greater than 1, the entire polyatomic ion must be enclosed in parentheses (brackets).

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Polyatomic Ion (Radical):** A charged chemical species composed of two or more atoms covalently bonded together.
- **Parentheses $()$:** Used in chemical formulas to group polyatomic ions when there is more than one present.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- Example: Combining Calcium ($Ca^{+2}$) with Hydroxide ($OH^-$). The 2 crosses over to the OH group, making $Ca(OH)_2$.
- Example: Combining Aluminum ($Al^{+3}$) with Sulfate ($SO_4^{-2}$). The criss-cross makes $Al_2(SO_4)_3$.
- DO NOT change the small subscript inside the radical (never change $SO_4$ to $SO_3$).

## What Students Should Remember
Look, forgetting the brackets is fatal. Writing $CaOH_2$ means one Calcium, one Oxygen, and two Hydrogens. Writing $Ca(OH)_2$ correctly means one Calcium and TWO Hydroxide groups (two Oxygens and two Hydrogens). Always use brackets for radicals if the criss-crossed number is 2 or more.
