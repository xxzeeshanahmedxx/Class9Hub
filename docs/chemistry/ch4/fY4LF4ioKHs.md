# Chemical Formula of Binary Ionic Compounds — Class 9 Chemistry Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This highly practical lecture teaches students the "Criss-Cross" method. The teacher explains exactly how to write the correct chemical formula for Binary Ionic Compounds using the valency of the elements.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
He introduces "Valency" as the combining capacity of an element (its charge as an ion). To write a formula for Aluminum Oxide, the teacher writes Al with its $+3$ valency and O with its $-2$ valency. Using the "Criss-Cross" rule, the number 3 moves to the bottom right of Oxygen, and the number 2 moves to the bottom right of Aluminum, resulting in the perfectly balanced formula $Al_2O_3$.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Binary Compound:** A chemical compound composed of exactly two different elements.
- **Valency (Oxidation State):** The electrical charge an atom acquires when it loses or gains electrons to form an ion.
- **Criss-Cross Method:** A trick used to determine the chemical formula of an ionic compound by crossing over the numerical values of the ionic charges.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- The positive ion (Cation/Metal) is ALWAYS written first (e.g., $Na$ before $Cl$).
- The negative ion (Anion/Non-metal) is written second.
- If the valencies are identical (e.g., $Mg^{+2}$ and $O^{-2}$), they cancel out, resulting in $MgO$, not $Mg_2O_2$.
- The signs ($+$ and $-$) are dropped when writing the final subscript numbers.

## What Students Should Remember
Honestly, ignoring the "cancel out" rule is the most common mistake. If you criss-cross Calcium ($+2$) and Carbonate ($-2$) and write $Ca_2(CO_3)_2$, it is wrong. Ionic formulas must be empirical (simplest ratio), so it must be simplified to $CaCO_3$.
