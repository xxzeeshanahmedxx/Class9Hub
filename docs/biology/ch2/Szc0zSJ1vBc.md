# Two and Three Kingdom Classification System — Class 9 Biology Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This lecture covers the earliest formal Kingdom models. The teacher explains the simplistic Two-Kingdom system and the subsequent Three-Kingdom system, highlighting the fatal flaws that forced scientists to keep updating the models.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Sir explains the oldest system: the Two-Kingdom system (Plantae and Animalia). Plants were autotrophs (made their own food) and didn't move; Animals were heterotrophs (ate food) and moved. However, organisms like *Euglena* had plant-like chlorophyll AND animal-like movement, breaking the rules. In 1866, Ernst Haeckel proposed a Three-Kingdom system, creating the kingdom Protista to house these weird, mixed organisms and bacteria.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Autotrophs:** Organisms that can synthesize their own food (e.g., plants).
- **Heterotrophs:** Organisms that cannot synthesize their own food and rely on other organisms (e.g., animals).
- **Protista:** The third kingdom introduced to house single-celled organisms that didn't fit into plants or animals.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- **Flaw of Two-Kingdom:** Fungi were put with plants, even though fungi are heterotrophs (they don't have chlorophyll).
- **Three-Kingdom addition:** Protista solved the *Euglena* problem.
- **Flaw of Three-Kingdom:** It still did not differentiate between Prokaryotes (cells without a nucleus, like bacteria) and Eukaryotes (cells with a nucleus).

## What Students Should Remember
So basically, examiners will definitely ask for the "Defects of the Two-Kingdom System." You must clearly state two things: First, it failed to classify organisms with dual traits (like Euglena). Second, it incorrectly grouped Fungi (heterotrophs) with Plants (autotrophs).
