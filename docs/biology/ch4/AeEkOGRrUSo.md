# Mitosis — Class 9 Biology Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Following Interphase, this lecture details the M-Phase: Mitosis. The teacher breaks down the exact physical process a cell uses to rip its copied DNA perfectly in half to create two identical clones.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The instructor divides Mitosis into Karyokinesis (division of the nucleus) and Cytokinesis (division of the cytoplasm). Karyokinesis has four strict phases: Prophase (chromosomes condense, nuclear envelope breaks), Metaphase (chromosomes line up perfectly in the middle/equator), Anaphase (spindle fibers rip the X-shaped chromosomes in half, pulling chromatids to opposite poles), and Telophase (two new nuclei form). 

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Mitosis:** A type of cell division that results in two daughter cells each having the exact same number and kind of chromosomes as the parent nucleus.
- **Karyokinesis:** The division of the nucleus during mitosis.
- **Cytokinesis:** The physical splitting of the cell's cytoplasm into two separate cells.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- **Prophase:** Preparation (DNA coils up, spindle fibers form).
- **Metaphase:** Alignment (Chromosomes align on the metaphase plate).
- **Anaphase:** Separation (Chromatids are pulled apart).
- **Telophase:** Reconstruction (Nuclear membranes reappear).
- Mitosis only occurs in Somatic (body) cells.

## What Students Should Remember
Pro tip: The mnemonic **PMAT** (Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase) is essential for remembering the correct sequence. Furthermore, examiners frequently ask what happens during Metaphase. You must describe the chromosomes aligning at the "equator" or "center" of the cell.
