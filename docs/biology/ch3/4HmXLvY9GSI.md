# Cell Wall — Class 9 Biology Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This lecture begins examining specific cellular structures, starting from teh outside in. The teacher focuses on the Cell Wall, an organelle present in plants, fungi, and bacteria, but notably absent in animals.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The instructor explains that the cell wall is a rigid, non-living protective layer located outside the cell membrane. It provides structural support, shape, and protection against osmotic bursting. The teacher details its chemical composition, which varies depending on the organism: plant cell walls are made of cellulose, fungi use chitin, and bacteria use peptidoglycan.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Cell Wall:** A rigid layer of polysaccharides lying outside the plasma membrane of the cells of plants, fungi, and bacteria.
- **Cellulose:** A tough, fibrous carbohydrate that forms the main component of plant cell walls.
- **Turgor Pressure:** The pressure exerted by fluid inside a plant cell against its cell wall, keeping the plant upright.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- The primary cell wall is thin and flexible (found in young growing plants).
- The secondary cell wall is thick and rigid (found in woody plants).
- Animal cells do NOT have a cell wall, allowing them to have flexible, changing shapes (like muscle cells).

## What Students Should Remember
Honestly, the chemical composition of the cell wall is the most frequently tested MCQ in this section. You guys must remember: Plants = Cellulose, Fungi = Chitin, Bacteria = Peptidoglycan (Murein). Mixing these up will cost you marks.
