# Cytoskeleton — Class 9 Biology Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This lecture covers the internal scaffolding of the cell: The Cytoskeleton. The teacher explains how cells maintain their shape and how internal organelles manage to move around without floating aimlessly.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The instructor explains that the cytoskeleton is a network of microscopic protein fibers that crisscross the entire cytoplasm. It is composed primarily of Microtubules (thick, hollow tubes made of tubulin protein) and Microfilaments (thinner, solid threads made of actin protein). These structures act like both the "bones" and the "muscles" of the cell, providing structural support and facilitating movement.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Cytoskeleton:** A microscopic network of protein filaments and tubules in the cytoplasm.
- **Microtubules:** Hollow protein tubes that maintain cell shape and form the spindle fibers during cell division.
- **Microfilaments:** Solid, thin protein threads that help the cell change its shape (like during muscle contraction or amoeba movement).

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- The cytoskeleton prevents the fragile cell membrane from collapsing inward.
- It provides "tracks" or "highways" along which organelles (like vesicles) are transported.
- It is highly dynamic, constantly breaking down and rebuilding itself as the cell moves.

## What Students Should Remember
To be fair, examiners usually ask for the protein composition of these structures. You must remember that Microtubules are made of the protein **Tubulin**, and Microfilaments are made of the protein **Actin**. Matching these correctly secures easy marks.
