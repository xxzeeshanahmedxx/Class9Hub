# Centrioles — Class 9 Biology Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
The final specific organelle discussed in this chapter is the Centriole. The teacher explores this unique structure that is found exclusively in animal cells, playing a critical role during cell reproduction.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The instructor describes centrioles as hollow, cylindrical structures made of nine triplets of microtubules. Sir explains that animal cells typically have two centrioles located near the nucleus (together called a Centrosome). When an animal cell prepares to divide (Mitosis), the centrioles duplicate and move to opposite poles of the cell. They then generate Spindle Fibers, the protein threads that grab and pull the chromosomes apart.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Centriole:** A minute cylindrical organelle near the nucleus in animal cells, occurring in pairs and involved in the development of spindle fibers in cell division.
- **Centrosome:** An organelle that is the main place where cell microtubules are organized; it contains the two centrioles.
- **Spindle Fibers:** The protein structure that divides the genetic material in a cell.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- Centrioles are ABSENT in higher plant cells.
- They are constructed from the exact same tubulin proteins found in the cytoskeleton.
- In some single-celled organisms, centrioles help form cilia and flagella (used for swimming).

## What Students Should Remember
Look, the absence of centrioles in plants is a major exam point. If you draw a plant cell in your board exam and accidentally draw centrioles near the nucleus, the examiner will mark it wrong. Plant cells manage to divide without them!
