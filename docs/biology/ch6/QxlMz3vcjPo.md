# Lipids — Class 9 Biology Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This lecture introduces the third group of biomolecules: Lipids (fats and oils). The teacher explains their unique chemical properties, specifically their absolute inability to mix with water.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Sir defines lipids as a diverse group of naturally occurring organic compounds that are Hydrophobic (water-fearing) and completely insoluble in water, but soluble in organic solvents (like ether or alcohol). The most common type is Triglycerides, which are built by attaching three Fatty Acid chains to one Glycerol molecule. The teacher distinguishes between Saturated fats (solid at room temperature, like butter) and Unsaturated fats (liquid oils).

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Lipids:** A broad group of hydrophobic organic compounds including fats, oils, and waxes.
- **Hydrophobic:** Water-repelling; unable to dissolve in water.
- **Triglyceride:** A lipid molecule made of one glycerol and three fatty acids.
- **Saturated Fat:** A lipid with only single carbon-carbon bonds, mostly found in animals (solid).
- **Unsaturated Fat:** A lipid containing one or more double bonds, mostly found in plants (liquid).

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- Lipids are extremely energy-dense, containing more than double the energy of carbohydrates per gram (9 kcal/g).
- Phospholipids (a type of lipid) are the primary building blocks of all cell membranes.
- Waxes protect plant leaves from losing water.

## What Students Should Remember
Listen carefully here: The building blocks of lipids are a guaranteed short question. Do NOT confuse them with proteins! You must state that a standard lipid (triglyceride) is built from "Fatty Acids and Glycerol."
