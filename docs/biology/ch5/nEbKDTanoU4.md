# Role of Organ Systems in Homeostasis — Class 9 Biology Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Following the definition of homeostasis, this lecture explores the practical mechanics. The teacher explains exactly *how* the different organ systems communicate and react to maintain internal balance.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The instructor introduces the "Feedback Mechanism," which involves three components: a Receptor (detects the change), a Control Center (the brain, which processes the info), and an Effector (the muscle or gland that takes action). Sir gives a classic example: If body temperature drops, skin receptors alert the brain. The brain acts as the control center and signals the muscles (effectors) to shiver. Shivering generates heat, raising the body temperature back to normal, at which point the shivering automatically stops (Negative Feedback).

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Negative Feedback Mechanism:** A control system that reverses a change in the internal environment to maintain homeostasis (e.g., cooling down when too hot).
- **Receptor:** A sensory organ or cell that detects a stimulus (change).
- **Effector:** An organ, gland, or muscle that becomes active in response to a signal from the control center.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- The Nervous System and Endocrine System (hormones) are the two primary control centers.
- The Kidneys act as effectors for Osmoregulation (producing more or less urine to balance water).
- The Skin and Muscles act as effectors for Thermoregulation (sweating and shivering).

## What Students Should Remember
Pro tip: Examiners love the shivering/sweating examples. If asked how the body cools itself, you must mention that sweat glands (effectors) release water onto the skin, which evaporates and absorbs heat, successfully lowering the body's internal temperature via negative feedback.
