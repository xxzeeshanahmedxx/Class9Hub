# Friction (Dissipative Effect, Sliding Friction) — Class 9 Physics Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
This lecture introduces Friction, the most common opposing force in nature. The teacher explains why objects slow down and stop without any apparent push, introducing friction as a "dissipative effect."

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The teacher explains that friction arises due to microscopic roughness (hills and valleys) on surfaces interlocking with each other. This creates "cold welds." The teacher defines Sliding Friction as the force resisting the sliding motion of one solid surface over another. 

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Friction:** The force that opposes the relative motion between two surfaces in contact.
- **Cold Welds:** Microscopic interlocking points between two surfaces that cause friction.
- **Sliding Friction:** The resistance encountered when an object slides over a surface.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- Friction always acts in the direction opposite to motion.
- Friction causes a "dissipative effect", meaning it converts kinetic energy into heat energy.
- Smoother surfaces have less friction, but friction can never be exactly zero.
- Static friction (stationary) is always greater than sliding friction (moving).

## What Students Should Remember
Actually, Friction is heavily dependent on the Normal Force. The heavier an object is, the harder the surfaces press together, making the cold welds stronger and thus increasing friction.
