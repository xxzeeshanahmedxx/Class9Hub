# Measurement of Atmospheric Pressure — Class 9 Physics Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Basically, this class explores the practical instruments used to measure the invisible weight of the air. The teacher focuses primarily on the Barometer, detailing its invention and functionality.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Our teacher explains Torricelli's experiment with the Mercury Barometer. A glass tube filled with mercury is inverted into a dish. The mercury column falls until its weight is perfectly balanced by the atmospheric pressure pushing down on the dish. At sea level, this height is exactly 76 cm (or 760 mm). 

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Barometer:** An instrument used specifically to measure atmospheric pressure.
- **Torricellian Vacuum:** The empty space containing zero air at the top of the barometer tube.
- **760 mm of Hg:** The standard measurement of atmospheric pressure at sea level.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- Why mercury? Mercury is 13.6 times denser than water.
- If a water barometer were used, the glass tube would need to be over 10 meters tall to balance atmospheric pressure.
- A sudden fall in barometric height indicates bad weather; a gradual rise indicates fair weather.

## What Students Should Remember
So basically, The reason mercury is used instead of water is a heavily tested conceptual question. You guys must state that mercury's high density allows the barometer to be a practical, manageable height (less than 1 meter).
