# Sensitivity, Range and Linearity of Thermometer — Class 9 Physics Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Basically, this class focuses on teh engineering specifications of measuring tools. The teacher explains the three characteristics that define the quality and suitability of a thermometer: Sensitivity, Range, and Linearity.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
Sir defines each term clearly. Sensitivity is how much the liquid expands for a small 1-degree change in temperature. Range is the span between the minimum and maximum measurable temperatures. Linearity refers to whether the liquid expands uniformly (the same distance per degree) across the entire scale, which is essential for an evenly marked thermometer.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Sensitivity:** The ability of a thermometer to detect small changes in temperature (large expansion per degree).
- **Range:** The temperature difference between the lower fixed point and the upper fixed point.
- **Linearity:** The uniform expansion of the thermometric liquid across the entire scale.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- To increase sensitivity, use a narrower capillary tube (the liquid is forced to travel further up).
- A clinical (medical) thermometer has high sensitivity but a very short range ($35^{\circ}\text{C}$ to $42^{\circ}\text{C}$).
- A laboratory thermometer has a wide range ($-10^{\circ}\text{C}$ to $110^{\circ}\text{C}$) but lower sensitivity.
- Mercury is highly linear, making calibration easy.

## What Students Should Remember
Honestly, There is an inverse trade-off in thermometer design. Increasing the sensitivity (by making the tube narrower) usually decreases the overall range, and vice versa. You guys must be able to justify why clinical thermometers are designed the way they are.
