# Micrometer Screw Gauge — Class 9 Physics Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Moving beyond the Vernier Calliper, this lecture introduces the Micrometer Screw Gauge. This tool is designed for extreme precision, used to measure microscopic objects like the thickness of a wire or a sheet of paper.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The teacher breaks down the anatomy of the Screw Gauge, consisting of a U-shaped frame, a stud, a spindle, and a thimble. The core mechanism is a precision screw. Sir explains the concept of "Pitch"—the distance moved by the spindle in one complete rotation. By dividing the pitch by the number of divisions on the circular scale, the teacher derives the Least Count of the instrument, showing it is 10 times more accurate than a Vernier Calliper.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Micrometer Screw Gauge:** A tool used to measure small lengths with an accuracy of 0.01 mm.
- **Pitch:** The distance between consecutive threads on the spindle (usually 0.5 mm or 1 mm).
- **Circular Scale:** The rotating scale on the thimble.
- **Linear/Main Scale:** The fixed scale on the hollow cylinder (sleeve).

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- Pitch = Distance moved in 1 rotation / number of rotations.
- Least Count = Pitch / Total circular scale divisions.
- Standard Least Count = 0.01 mm (or 0.001 cm).
- Always use the ratchet to avoid applying too much pressure on the object.

## What Students Should Remember
Well, Remember the Least Count of the Screw Gauge: 0.01 mm. The teacher emphasized the importance of the ratchet; tightening the gauge from the thimble instead of the ratchet can damage the instrument and cause erroneous readings.
