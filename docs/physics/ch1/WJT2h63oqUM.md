# Uncertainty in a Measurement — Class 9 Physics Lecture Summary

## Lecture Overview
Following the topic of errors, this lecture dives into Uncertainty in Measurement. The teacher explains that uncertainty is an inherent limitation of measuring tools. This concept teaches students that every scientific reading falls within a specific range rather than being an absolute, exact point.

## Main Concepts Explained by the Teacher
The teacher links Uncertainty directly to the Least Count of an instrument. For instance, a meter rule with a least count of 1 mm cannot give an exact reading smaller than 1 mm; therefore, any measurement has a built-in uncertainty of $\pm 1$ mm. The teacher explains that absolute uncertainty is simply equal to the least count of the measuring device being used.

## Important Definitions and Terms
- **Uncertainty:** The margin of doubt that exists for any given measurement.
- **Absolute Uncertainty:** The maximum possible error in a measurement, which is equal to the least count of the instrument.
- **Fractional Uncertainty:** The absolute uncertainty divided by the measured value.

## Key Points and Lists from the Lecture
- No physical measurement is perfectly exact; there is always a degree of uncertainty.
- Uncertainty = $\pm$ Least Count.
- Example: Measuring with a ruler (Least count 0.1 cm) means a reading of 5.4 cm is actually $5.4 \pm 0.1$ cm.
- Better instruments (smaller least count) result in lower uncertainty.

## What Students Should Remember
Actually, Students really need to grasp that writing $5.4 \pm 0.1$ cm means the true value lies somewhere strictly between 5.3 cm and 5.5 cm. To decrease uncertainty and improve data quality, one must use a more precise instrument, like switching from a meter rule to a Vernier calliper.
